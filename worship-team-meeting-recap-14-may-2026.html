PART 1 Bible Study

The Battle Belongs To The Lord.

2 Chronicles 20:15 "The battle is not yours, but God's."

The first part of the meeting was the heart of it. We went around the call and each said one thing we're thankful for at Calvary, then opened the Bible.

The story was Jehoshaphat in 2 Chronicles 20. Three armies marching toward Judah. One king, outnumbered. He prayed honestly — "We don't know what to do, but our eyes are on you." God's answer, through a man called Jahaziel: "The battle is not yours, but God's."

Then God did something strange. He told Jehoshaphat to put singers at the front of the army. Singers, not soldiers. The moment they began to sing, God set ambushes. The three armies turned on each other. By the time Judah's soldiers arrived, the battle was already won.

That's what we do on Sunday. We go first. We sing. God does the fighting.

We also touched on Gideon — God shrank his army from 32,000 men down to 300, so nobody could say they'd won the battle themselves — and Jericho, where God's people walked around the city in silence for six days and shouted on the seventh, and the walls just fell. Same God in all three. Same pattern. He uses what looks impossible so it's obvious He's the one who did it.

Then we talked about four things we want to keep growing in as a team:

On the third one, Bolaji talked about Malcolm Gladwell's 10,000 hours idea — that anyone can get really good at anything if they practise enough. That's not just for music. When you get skilled at something, doors open. That's what Proverbs 22:29 is talking about.

PART 2 Upgrades

A New Standard.

Colossians 3:23 "Whatever you do, work at it with all your heart."

Sunday is going to look and sound different. The church has invested in some real upgrades:

Visuals. New backdrops and banners at the back of the stage — a proper facelift for the room. A new camera so the livestream looks like real broadcast quality instead of phone quality.

Audio. A new multicore cable — that's the big cable with lots of plugs that runs from the stage to the sound desk — with more channels so we can plug in everything we need. A pile of new XLR cables for microphones. And — new for the singers — an in-ear monitor system, like what the band members already use. It'll feel different in your ears the first time. We test it Saturday.

Bolaji's framing on all this: Calvary is investing in Calvary. The church is putting real money into making Sunday better — which means we should bring our best too. Show up prepared, look the part, sing like it matters. Because it does.

PART 3 Set Structure

Boundaries Make You Free.

Joshua 1:8 "Meditate on it day and night."

The praise and worship set is short — about 20 to 30 minutes. That means we can keep it tight and repeatable instead of changing songs every week. The same set, refined again and again, gets stronger every Sunday.

There's a practical reason too. Our band is smaller right now. If we lock in a tight, repeatable structure, we can add backing tracks underneath — pre-recorded drums or bass or keyboard pads — so the sound is full even when we're missing players. The catch: once we're playing along to a backing track, we can't change the structure on the fly. Everyone has to know exactly where the song is going.

So for now: lock in praise tightly, leave worship more open for the Spirit. Both improve over time, but in different ways.

"When you add boundaries, it makes you become more free. If I know the structure, I can sing around it. I can do my ad-libs. But I just know what's happening in the background." Bolaji, on structure as a gift

This is the Repetition pillar from Part 1, in action. Sunday should feel like the hundredth time. Not the first.

The thing worth dwelling on is the vocals. Normally a worship team has one lead singer at the front and backing singers behind. This Sunday is different. All four singers can lead. Uncle Caster has done it for years. Esther — at twelve, our youngest — can carry a song. Uncle Shola is a professional singer who's led bands. Auntie Jess sings strongly.

Bolaji's framing: think of the four voices as one strong vocal section, not a hierarchy. Whoever's leading any given song is just the one telling the church where to go next. Everyone else fills out the sound. It's a richer, fuller texture than a traditional lead-and-backing setup.

We test the arrangement Saturday at rehearsal.

PART 5 Set Walkthrough

The Set.

Worship Pack · 17 May 2026 The full set with direction and reference audio.

The last part of the meeting was the working session — song by song, real-time. This is where the set actually got built. A few of the decisions were already settled going in; others got made live.

The praise set stays as we've been doing it: Thanks Thanks I Give You Thanks as the a cappella opener, then Trading My Sorrows → Lord You Are Good → Friend of God. Direction is largely the same as the last few Sundays — just tighter.

Every Praise is out. We needed room for something new, and the song that took its place is Open the Eyes of My Heart — but in an arrangement none of us have done before. Bolaji played a reference that has a different feel: laid-back, groove-driven, almost dance-floor underneath the vocals. Different from how Calvary normally sounds. The praise set will end with a hard stop on Friend of God, then we'll start Open the Eyes of My Heart fresh.

Open the Eyes into worship is the trickier transition. The plan: drop the beat entirely and go fresh into My Jesus My Saviour, with Esther leading. There's a chance we try My Jesus on the same upbeat groove if Esther wants to lead it that way — we'll experiment Saturday.

"I'm pushing the boundary. Give it a try. If it sounds better, go with it. If it doesn't, we just scrub it." Bolaji, on the new Open the Eyes arrangement

Here I Am to Worship is out. The decision was that for a celebration Sunday, we want the worship set to stay more upbeat — not drop into traditionally solemn songs. Replacing it: Come Now Is the Time to Worship and Because of Who You Are, both of which can carry energy without losing depth.

Offering isn't a band-led song. Pastor Shade has invited Evangelist Bola Paul, who'll do an Afro praise set with a talking drum. The band accompanies her, doesn't lead.

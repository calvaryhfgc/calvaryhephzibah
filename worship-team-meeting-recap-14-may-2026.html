PART 1 Bible Study

The Battle Belongs To The Lord.

2 Chronicles 20:15 "The battle is not yours, but God's."

The first part of the meeting was the heart of it. After a short round-table where each of us shared a reason to be thankful, we opened the Bible.

The story was Jehoshaphat in 2 Chronicles 20. Three armies marching toward Judah. One king, outnumbered. He prayed honestly — "We don't know what to do, but our eyes are on you." God's answer, through a man called Jahaziel: "The battle is not yours, but God's."

Then God did something strange. He told Jehoshaphat to put singers at the front of the army. Singers, not soldiers. The moment they began to sing, God set ambushes. The three armies turned on each other. By the time Judah's soldiers arrived, the battle was already won.

The point landed: that's what we do on Sunday. We go first. We sing. God does the fighting.

We also touched on Gideon — 32,000 men reduced to 300, so God's people couldn't claim the victory as their own — and Jericho, where walking around the city in silence for six days and a shout on the seventh brought the walls down. Same God in all three. Same pattern. He uses what looks impossible so the credit goes where it belongs.

PART 2 Upgrades

A New Standard.

Colossians 3:23 "Whatever you do, work at it with all your heart."

Sunday is going to look and sound different. We've invested in real upgrades across the board:

Visuals. New backdrops and banners at the back of the stage — a proper facelift for the room. A new camera so the broadcast looks closer to broadcast quality than phone quality.

Audio. A new multicore cable with more channels so we have room to plug in everything we need. A pile of new XLRs. And — new for the singers — an in-ear system similar to what the instrumentalists have been using. It'll feel different. We'll test it on Saturday.

The framing Bolaji put on all this: Calvary is investing in Calvary. The technical lift comes with a quiet expectation — if the broadcast is going to look this good, we put our best foot forward too. Spirit of excellence in everything, not just the sound.

PART 3 Set Structure

Boundaries Make You Free.

Joshua 1:8 "Meditate on it day and night."

The praise-and-worship set is short. 20 to 30 minutes, by most measures. That means we can — and should — keep it tight and repeatable rather than chopping and changing each week. The same set, refined again and again, gets stronger every Sunday.

There's a practical reason too. Our band is smaller than it has been. A repeatable structure lets us add programmed elements — drum tracks, bass tracks, keyboard pads — so the sound fills out even when not every chair is filled. The compromise: once you're locked into a structured arrangement, you can't deviate without it falling apart.

So for now: lock in praise tightly, leave worship more open for the Spirit. Both improve over time, but in different ways.

"When you add boundaries, it makes you become more free. If I know the structure, I can sing around it. I can do my ad-libs. But I just know what's happening in the background." Bolaji, on structure as a gift

This is the Repetition pillar from Part 1, applied. Sunday should feel like the hundredth time. Not the first.

The thing worth dwelling on is the vocals. Four strong singers — not the usual one lead and a wall of backing. Bolaji pitched it as vocals-as-instrument: think of the four of you as a single, strong vocal section, evenly arranged on stage, each capable of leading. Leading then becomes a question of direction — who's telling the church where to go next — rather than hierarchy.

Uncle Shola is a professional singer. Auntie Jess sings very well. With Uncle Caster's experience and Esther's voice, this is a strong group. We test the arrangement Saturday.

PART 5 Set Walkthrough

The Set.

Worship Pack · 17 May 2026 The full set with direction and reference audio.

The last part of the meeting was the working session — song by song, real-time. This is where the set actually got built. A few of the decisions were already settled going in; others got made live.

The praise set stays as we've been doing it: Thanks Thanks I Give You Thanks as the a cappella opener, then Trading My Sorrows → Lord You Are Good → Friend of God. Direction is largely the same as the last few Sundays — just tighter.

Every Praise is out. We needed room for something new, and the song that took its place is Open the Eyes of My Heart — but in an arrangement none of us have done before. Bolaji played a reference that leans soulful house, rare groove, with a Latin-flavoured "I'm a piano" loop underneath. Different feel for Calvary. The praise set will end with a hard stop on Friend of God, then we'll start Open the Eyes of My Heart fresh.

Open the Eyes into worship is the trickier transition. The plan: drop the beat entirely and go fresh into My Jesus My Saviour, with Esther leading. There's a chance we try My Jesus on the same Afro-leaning beat if Esther wants to lead it that way — we'll experiment Saturday.

"I'm pushing the boundary. Give it a try. If it sounds better, go with it. If it doesn't, we just scrub it." Bolaji, on the new Open the Eyes arrangement

Here I Am to Worship is out. The decision was that for a celebration Sunday, we want the worship set to stay more upbeat — not drop into traditionally solemn songs. Replacing it: Come Now Is the Time to Worship and Because of Who You Are, both of which can carry energy without losing depth.

Offering isn't a band-led song. Pastor Shade has invited Evangelist Bola Paul, who'll do an Afro praise set with a talking drum. The band accompanies her, doesn't lead.

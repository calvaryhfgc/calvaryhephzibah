Calvary OS · Control Room · Product Brief

From Sunday loop to service engine

Why Control Room needs to grow up — and what the 34th Anniversary on 17 May is teaching us about its current shape.
Author: Bolaji Olatoye · Chief of Staff Date: 13 May 2026 Companion: 17 May Running Order
01 — Where we are

Control Room is a Sunday-shaped tool

Control Room was built quickly and well, against a specific brief: take the chaos out of a normal Sunday service. It does that. Today it can hold a setlist, push lyrics in time, search and display scripture, switch backdrops, dim a logo, and run a small library of announcements. Scott can do most of what the in-room screens need from a phone, and the OBS overlay receives those changes in real time.

But the brief that shaped it — a normal Sunday — is now also its ceiling. Every feature in the operator app assumes a single, fixed rhythm: welcome, worship, scripture, announcements, sermon, offering, close. Lyrics live next to scripture next to graphics next to backdrops. There is no concept of "this service is different to last week." There is no concept of slides as content. There is no concept of a presentation moving forward at its own pace.

That was the right thing to ship first. It is no longer enough.

02 — Where it falls short

The anniversary makes the shape of the gap visible

Sunday 17 May is the 34th Anniversary. The order of service is 15 items long, runs four hours, and breaks the normal rhythm at several points. Communion happens at the tables in the first half. A guest evangelist ministers in song while the offering is taken. Pastor Shade calls long-serving members by name in a Show of Appreciation. Departments bring short presentations dotted through the running order. The service ends with a sit-down meal.

None of these are exotic. They are the same kinds of moments that show up in any special service: weddings, baby dedications, memorials, Easter, Christmas, leadership inductions, conferences, women's services, men's services, harvest, watch-night. The anniversary just makes them all visible at once.

If we walk Scott through the day with what Control Room can do today, the picture is uncomfortable. He can run the worship block cleanly. He can push scripture for the bible reading. After that, every distinctive moment forces him out of the operator app and into improvisation: pre-baked JPGs flying through Graphics → Images, scrambling to find the right one in real time, with no preview, no order, no recovery if Pastor Shade calls a name out of sequence.

The problem isn't that any one feature is missing. It's that Control Room has no language for "this service is different to a normal Sunday."
03 — The diagnosis

Three primitives Control Room doesn't have

Strip away the long list of individual gaps and three things sit underneath all of them. These are the architectural pieces missing from v1.

Primitive 01
Special Services
A first-class concept of a service that isn't a normal Sunday. Each one has its own order of service, its own visual language, its own categories of overlay. Anniversaries, weddings, dedications, memorials, Easter, Christmas, conferences. Today everything assumes "Sunday." Tomorrow the operator app should ask: which kind of service is this, and load the right shape accordingly.
Primitive 02
Slides
A general slide concept. Today Control Room has lyric slides and scripture slides, but no notion of "slide" as a generic primitive. A sermon deck, a department's photo carousel, a Show of Appreciation sequence of named cards, a wedding photo retrospective — these are all slides. Once slides exist as a primitive, all of them become the same kind of object with different content.
Primitive 03
Presentations
A presenter walking through prepared content at their own pace. A department contribution, a testimony, a guest's introduction, a ministry update, a deck handed in on Friday. Today there is no "presentation mode" — no place to upload a deck, no slide-by-slide advance, no countdown for the 3-minute cap. Presentations are where Control Room has the largest blind spot.

Almost every individual gap in the anniversary briefing — name cards, lower-thirds, slide uploads, countdown timers, communion sequences, meal-time galleries — collapses into one of these three. Build the primitives properly and the symptoms resolve.

04 — Where we're going

Control Room v2 — same app, bigger brain

Control Room v2 is not a rewrite. It's the same operator app and the same overlay, with three new ideas baked in.

Concept
Today
v2
Service shape
Hardcoded Sunday order of service. Same panels regardless of what's happening on stage.
Operator picks a Service Type at the top of the session. Anniversary, Wedding, Memorial, Easter, etc. The order of service, the available overlay categories, and the default visual style all shift to match.
Visual content
Lyrics, scripture, fixed graphic categories (announcements, backdrops). Everything else is fullscreen JPG.
A unified Slides primitive. Lyric slides, scripture slides, photo slides, named-person slides, sermon slides, announcement slides — all the same kind of object, each with its own template. Operator queues a deck, advances slide-by-slide.
Presentations
No concept at all. Coordinators send slides as JPGs; Scott loads them into Graphics → Images and improvises on the day.
Presentation mode: a coordinator uploads a deck ahead of the service (PPT, PDF, or images), it's parsed into slides automatically, queued against its slot in the order of service. On the day Scott picks it from the running order and advances slides as the presenter speaks. Built-in countdown timer enforces the slot length.
Order of service
Display-only. Shows the operator what's next; doesn't drive anything else.
Operational spine. Each item has a type (worship, scripture, presentation, named-honour, offering, meal, etc.). Tapping an item loads the right operator panel for that type. The order of service stops being a reference and becomes the cockpit.
Person library
No concept. Names appear by typing them into ad-hoc graphics.
A stored library of people — name, role, photo, default honour line. Used for lower-thirds when someone speaks, named-honour slides when someone is appreciated, sermon attribution. One source of truth, used everywhere.
Visual style
One fixed look across all services.
Service-type-aware. The Sunday look stays. Anniversary opens up the brand red. Memorial uses a more subdued treatment. Wedding gets a warm cream variant. Each service type pulls from the existing Calvary design system tokens but applies a different mood.

06 — Sequencing

What ships when

v2 is real work. The question is what's in by 17 May, and what's after. Honest sequencing:

Before 17 May · 4 days
Minimum to make Sunday work
  • Manually load anniversary slides into the existing Images panel — name cards, communion graphics, offering bank details, meal-time backdrop
  • Scott rehearses the sequence Saturday evening
  • MC holds a physical stopwatch for presentation timing
  • Coordinators send slides as JPGs by Friday
June–July · post-anniversary
v2 phase 1 — Slides + Person library
  • Slides primitive in the data model — supersedes the current ad-hoc Images model
  • Person library in Supabase with photos and roles
  • Named-honour slide template (the Show of Appreciation pattern, generalised)
  • Lower-third overlay region
Q3 2026 · groundwork for next anniversary
v2 phase 2 — Service types + Presentations
  • Service Type selector at the top of the session
  • Visual style variants per service type
  • Presentation mode: deck upload, parsing, slot binding, countdown timer
  • Order of Service as operational spine — tap an item, open the right panel

This sequence keeps Sunday 17 May safe — Scott runs it with what he has, with extra rehearsal. Then the proper architectural work happens in the months after, with the anniversary as the requirements document for v2. By the time the next major service lands — a wedding, an Easter, the 35th — Control Room is a different tool.

07 — Why this matters

The investment compounds

Calvary runs special services regularly. The anniversary every May. Easter and Christmas every year. Weddings and dedications when they come. Conferences, women's services, men's services, the leadership-induction services that are coming. Each of them currently demands the same hand-built improvisation that 17 May is about to demand. Each time we pay the cost from scratch.

Control Room v2 doesn't just solve the anniversary. It solves the shape of every special service we'll ever run. The work happens once; the value compounds across every Sunday and every special service for years.

The brief in front of us is: get through 17 May with what we have, then build the right thing.

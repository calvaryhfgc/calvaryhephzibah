The story

Jehoshaphat

Jehoshaphat was a king. One day, messengers ran in and told him three huge armies were marching toward his country. Three. He had one small army. He was outnumbered, and he was scared.

He could have panicked. Instead, he called the whole nation together — men, women, children — and prayed. He stood up in front of everyone and said, basically, "God, we don't know what to do. But we're looking to you."

And God answered. He spoke through a man called Jahaziel:

2 Chronicles 20:15
"Do not be afraid… for the battle is not yours, but God's."

Then Jehoshaphat did something strange. He put singers at the front of the army. Not soldiers — singers. The instruction was: walk out ahead, sing to God, praise Him.

And here's the bit I want you to hear:

2 Chronicles 20:22
"As they began to sing and praise, the Lord set ambushes against the men… and they were defeated."

The moment the singers started singing, God moved. The three armies turned on each other. By the time Judah's soldiers showed up, the battle was already done. They didn't lift a sword.

Read that again. The singers won the battle. Not by being loud, not by being good — by going first, in faith, while God did the fighting.

That's what we do on Sunday. We go first. We sing. God does the fighting.

And here's something worth holding onto: sometimes God lets us end up in situations that feel impossible on purpose. If Jehoshaphat had a bigger army than the three coming at him, he'd have just won the fight and gone home. He'd have thought it was his own doing. By making it impossible, God made sure everyone knew who actually won.

The same thing happens with us. The moments we feel out of our depth — a song we're not sure we can pull off, a part we're still learning, a Sunday where the room feels too big — those aren't bad moments. They're reminders. We're not in control. He is. And that's good news, not scary news.

Gideon

Judges 6–7

Gideon was the least important son in the least important family in his tribe. When the angel showed up and called him "mighty warrior," Gideon was literally hiding in a winepress trying not to get robbed. He didn't think he was the guy.

But God told him to lead an army against the Midianites — a people who'd been bullying Israel for seven years. Gideon gathered 32,000 men. Outnumbered, but doable.

Then God said something strange: "You have too many men." He told Gideon to send anyone home who was scared. 22,000 left. Down to 10,000. God said: still too many. Sent them to drink from a stream, kept only the ones who drank a certain way. 300 left.

300 men. Against an army the Bible says was "as thick as locusts." Gideon must have thought he was going to die.

God's plan: no swords. Just trumpets, clay jars with torches inside, and a shout. In the middle of the night they surrounded the camp, smashed the jars to reveal the torches, blew the trumpets, and yelled. The Midianites panicked, turned on each other in the dark, and ran.

And God said it out loud — He told Gideon why He kept cutting the army down: "Lest Israel claim the win for themselves." God made it impossible on purpose, so no one could say "we did this." There was no other explanation.

Jericho

Joshua 6

After Moses died, Joshua led Israel into the land God had promised them. The first city in their way was Jericho — and Jericho was a fortress. Massive walls. The city had locked its gates and was ready to wait out a siege. From the outside, there was no obvious way in.

God gave Joshua an instruction that sounded ridiculous. No battering rams. No siege engines. No attack at all.

Instead: march around the city once a day, for six days. Carry the Ark of the Covenant. Have seven priests walk in front of it, blowing trumpets. Don't say a word. Just walk.

So they did. Day one — march around, go home. Day two — same thing. The people of Jericho must have been watching from the walls thinking these guys were strange. Day three. Day four. Day five. Day six.

On day seven, the instruction changed. March around seven times. Then, when the priests blow a long blast on the trumpets, everyone shout.

They did. They shouted. And the walls collapsed — not chipped, not breached. Flat. The city was theirs.

No human plan would have worked. The point wasn't the marching. The point was: God breaks down walls. Our job was to trust Him enough to keep walking when it didn't make sense.

Three different stories. Same point. God doesn't change. He fought for Jehoshaphat. He fought for Gideon. He fought at Jericho. He's been fighting for Calvary for 34 years. He's not going to stop on Sunday.

So whatever you're nervous about — a part you're still learning, a moment you might mess up — bring it to the meeting. We'll work it out together. The battle isn't yours.

What we're growing in

Four Things

These are the four things I want us to keep coming back to as a team. Not just for Sunday. For everything we do together going forward.

1.Know where you're going

If you don't know where you're going, it's easy to wander. As a team, we want to know what we're building — what we sound like, what we look like, what kind of musicians and singers we're becoming. Take a minute this week and picture it. What does the version of you in two years look like? What are they playing? How do they show up?

Proverbs 29:18 (KJV)
"Where there is no vision, the people perish."

2.Don't half-do it

When we play and sing, we're doing it for God. That means the prep matters. Learning your part properly matters. Showing up on time matters. Tuning your instrument before service starts matters. Excellence isn't about being perfect — it's about caring enough to give your best.

Colossians 3:23
"Whatever you do, work at it with all your heart, as working for the Lord."

3.Keep learning

None of us are finished. Not the youngest, not the oldest. The Bible says if you're skilled at what you do, you end up in front of important people — not because you tried to, but because skill opens doors. Practise your part this week. Learn one new thing. Ask a question if you're stuck. That's how it works.

Proverbs 22:29
"Do you see someone skilled in their work? They will serve before kings."

4.Do it again, and again

You don't get good at something by doing it once. You get good by doing it over and over until your body knows it before your brain does. That's true for a drum fill, a vocal run, a chord change, a transition between songs. Repeat it. Then repeat it again. Sunday should feel like the hundredth time, not the first.

Joshua 1:8
"Meditate on it day and night, so you may be careful to do everything written in it."

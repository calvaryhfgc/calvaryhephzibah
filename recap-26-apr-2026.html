Things to work on

Song structure — Lord You Are Good

Structure

The agreed structure from rehearsal didn't get followed cleanly. Mid-song, Caster wasn't sure how many verse/chorus repeats — the band ended up guessing where he was going. At one point one part of the band heard a "you are good" cue and started playing the bridge while others continued the verse.

Fix: see the MD section below — this is a vocalising-out-loud problem more than a structure problem.

Friend of God ending

Ending cleanly

The end of Friend of God was the messiest moment of the set — number of rounds wasn't agreed in the moment, count was lost, some played four rounds, some played three, some two. It was visible to the congregation.

It was messy and it looked messy. Everybody looked confused.

Fix: end on an even number of rounds. Once you've done four times, end the song. If you miss the cue, you miss it — but commit to ending. Don't drift.

Most songs you don't sing a section an odd number of times — if you do, the band most likely will miss the cue. So just be assertive about when you're ending. Uncle Mike

Trading My Sorrows → Afro switch

Style change

The plan was to switch to Afro on the fourth repeat. By the time Caster called "sing it one more time", the switch hadn't happened — too late to change style by then.

Fix: if Laolu has the cue in his head ("we switch on the fourth"), he vocalises it out loud at the right moment so the rest of the band knows. Don't keep the cue silent.

Toms — pull back on the build

Drums

Coming in too early on the toms during builds — specifically "God Almighty" in Friend of God. That section needs to build: pull the toms back, let it grow, then bring them in.

When Laolu was in time, he was in sync and added well. The note is about restraint on the build, not about the playing itself.

Volume balance — keys + bass

Mix

Keys were too loud at points. The mixer couldn't be accessed from the floor because too many devices were connected — only one person can control the desk at a time with the current setup, and that wasn't working out cleanly today.

Bass was quiet — Bolaji A had to switch to passive when his battery died, increased the gain, but it wasn't enough to compensate for the loss of active signal.

Fix: the MD on a given Sunday has the desk. If anyone else is going to tweak from the floor, that needs to be agreed upfront, not improvised.

Morayo — playing styles

Guitar

Heard clearly during Friend of God when playing variations, but drowned out elsewhere — keys are also harmonically strings and will swallow chord-based guitar.

Coaching note from Uncle Mike: most worship songs are 1-4-5. Learn typical rock or Congolese-style solo phrases — they're usually just 1-4-5 played fancifully. Once you've got a few patterns, you can drop them into almost any song or section as flavour. It's not about overcomplicating; it's about adding something other than chords.

It's just flavour. And that's it. It doesn't overcomplicate. Uncle Mike

Esther — projection & confidence

Backing vocal

Mic was quiet at the start. The note was that she sings softly and there's an element of fear that's understandable — she needs to project from the diaphragm rather than the throat.

The team's view: this is teachable, not innate. Suggestion was for her dad to help, plus videos to work through. The path forward is gentle and supportive — she's doing well overall.

Caster — enunciation

Lead vocal

Words like magnified and apostrophe contractions like God's are hard given English is Caster's third language — God's tends to come out as God is, and magnified gets weighted on the wrong syllables.

The framing: this is solvable and teachable. Worth a small bit of focused work between Sundays on the lyric sheets where these specific words appear.

The MD role

One MD per service. Vocalised, not implicit.

Recurring theme

The biggest single takeaway threading through the gaps above: service flow needs one MD whose voice the band follows. Church is fluid — the worship leader will make in-the-moment calls. The MD's job is to say it out loud so everyone else hears the call: "verse, verse, chorus, chorus", "we're looping", "switch to Afro", "this is the last round."

You've got to be the voice. Sometimes you might not have picked it up — once you've picked it up from say the drummer, you're like right, verse verse chorus chorus. That's where we're going. Uncle Mike

If the cue is in someone's head, it doesn't help the band. Vocalise it. The same principle applies to the desk — the MD has the mixer; if anyone else is going to control sound from the floor, agree it before service, don't improvise.

Three of the gaps above (Lord You Are Good structure, Friend of God ending, the missed Afro switch) are fundamentally one problem expressed three different ways: cues that lived inside one person's head and didn't make it to the rest of the band.
